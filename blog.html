10th of December 2017

As for the out-tunnelling current, the measurement in the paper is set up such that as soon as an electron tunnels in, the gate voltage is raised (through a feedback mechanism), and the it stays up until the electron on the dot jumps out. The out-tunnelling for the first level is small because only one electron is going to jump out. This results in the 2:1 ratio seen in the upper leftmost plot in Fig. 2. Now, when we add the second electron, it will land in the ground state, with a spin that is opposite to the spin of the electron already on the dot. The ground state, which can accomodate two electrons, already has one electron in it, and so has room for one more, i.e., the probability that an electron will tunnel is halved from when we were adding the first electron. As soon as the second electron jumps on, the feedback mechanism raises the gate energy and waits for the electrons to tunnel out. There are two of them so an electrons is twice as likley to tunnel out that it was to tunnel in, hence the 1:2 ratio between in-tunnelling and out-tunnelling current.

8- What is the weak linear energy dependence mention in the paper?: From Fig. 2, we see that the tunneling rates are not constant as we vary the energy near the extremes. For a quantum dot with sharply defined energy levels, one would expect the tunneling rates to be constant: if the energy of the dot falls below that of the source, an electron will tunnel in with a certain probability that does not depend on how far below the source energy the dot energy is; and if dot energy goes above the drain energy, an electron will tunnel out with a certain probability, not matter how far above the drain energy the dot energy is. Where does the energy dependence come from, then? It comes from the fact that the tunnelling rate depends exponentially on the energy of the electron on and off the dot (Vds and Vgs), as can be seen from the WKB formula. For small variatons in the energies, the depends can be approximated by a linear model, which can be seen in the paper.

9- What happens when we turn on a magnetic field? And how can this be seen from Fig. 3?: The spin degeneracy will be lifted, with a Zeeman energy splitting. This can be seen from Fig. 3 by seeing that at small negative energies, an electron is as likely to tunnel as it is to tunnel out, indicating that the lowest level can accomodate only one electron (with a spin parallel to the magnetic field). When the energy is made more negative such that the excited Zeeman level goes below the source energy, there is room for two electrons to tunnel in, which manifests as a two-fold increase in the in-tunneling rate.

13th of December 2017

So...why do I seem so convinced that a photon is at its core a particle, albeit one that nature forces to behave like a wave (when it is isolated from interactions with the environment) for some reason. The short answer is that its a hunch. Of course it's a hunch. But here is how that hunch came to grow. It started with the following gedanken experiment: suppose we have two balls of steel the size of earth on a collision course in deep space. Suppose now that you have a camera that can take a snapshot of the scene, just before the balls collide. The camera does not capture photons, but rather works by detecting the gravitational disturbance of the balls on spacetime. How can someone who is given the picture of the gravometric disturbance around the balls at a single moment in time determine whether the balls are going to undergo a massive collision, or are just going to sit there? The answer is that he/she can check whether there are doppler-like spacetime compressions around the balls, and if there are compressions, use them to determine their velocities relative to a stationary observer, and consequently their energies, and whether they are going to smash into each other or not. The smaller the momentum of either of the steel balls is, the less compressed spacetime in its direction of motion will be. Now take one of the balls and slow it down just enough for it to have a deBroglie wavelength long enough that the ball will exhibit diffraction at a slit that has a width equal to its diameter (steel might not be the material at which that is possible...it might be worthwhile to calculate the required density of the ball. Might have to be a blackhole! Exciting! Anything with "blackhole" in it is exciting. EDIT: the bigger the ball, the less dense it will have to be [\( \rho = {6h \over \pi d^4 v} \), where d is the diameter of the ball and v its velocity; this calculation is for \( \lambda = d\)]). Now, at that momentum value, what is the length scale of the spacetime compressions? Is it on the order of magnitude of some famous length scale in quantum information theory?

It is important to keep in mind that spacetime compressions are relative, i.e., they change from reference frame to another. That means we can control the wavelength of the ball as it goes through the slit by making the slit move with a constant velocity towards the ball in space (what happens in the slit accelerates?). If the slit is moving towards the ball too fast, the deBroglie wavelength of the ball, as seen by the slit, will be too short for diffraction.

Here's another thread of thought: imagine we take one of the steel balls and make sure, to the best of our abilities (we can not interact with it, otherwise its quantum state will collapse), that it is has 0 velocity. From a snapshot using the gravometric camera we used earlier, noone, including nature, can ever tell where the ball will go next, because it's not moving. Now let's consider the same ball but now it has a huge momentum (very short wavelength). We know more or less exactly where it's going. Even as it passes a narrow slit, it will not diffract very much. Now...why did we assume that these two are the only states of knowledge (not knowing at all where the ball will go next, and knowing more or less exactly where it will go next) that we can have about the system? Why did we assume that the states of knowledge that we can have about the system is not a continuum?

For a stationary ball, we cannot tell where it will go next. For a ball with very high momentum, we can tell exactly where it will go next. Assuming that the possible knowledge that we can gain about the system is a continuum, then the following is valid for a ball with tiny momentum: whatever knowledge that we can gain about the ball without disturbing it in any way will only tell us that that ball will travel in the direction of its tiny velocity, BUT it might not travel exactly in that direction; it could go a bit left, or a bit right.

Calculating the squashed entanglement of a state is NP-hard (Huang 2013). In (K. Goodenough et al. 2016), tight upper bounds on squashed entanglement of channels were explored by picking a fixed squashing channel (they use primarily the identity squashing channel). Is it possible to use a machine learning model to find a squashing channel that results in a tighter bound than those resulting from the squashing channels in (K. Goodenough et al. 2016)?

Finding a good squashing channel is an optimization problem. Since it is provably an NP-Hard problem, the best you can do is do an inefficient search for a good squashing channel; and you would have to do this for every channel you want to calculate the squashed entanglement for.
